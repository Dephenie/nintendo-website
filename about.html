History of Nintendo

From playing cards to global gaming giant

Nintendo was founded in 1889 by Fusajiro Yamauchi in Kyoto, Japan. The company originally made traditional Japanese playing cards called Hanafuda. For a long time, that's all they did.

In the 1970s, Nintendo started experimenting with electronics and eventually entered the video game market. Their early arcade games like Donkey Kong helped them get noticed internationally, especially in the US.

The NES (Famicom) launched in 1983 and basically saved the video game industry after the 1983 crash. Games like Super Mario Bros. and The Legend of Zelda became iconic and set the standard for what video games could be.

Since then, Nintendo has kept releasing new consoles — Game Boy, SNES, N64, GameCube, Wii, DS, 3DS, and eventually the Nintendo Switch in 2017. They've always focused on making games fun rather than just chasing the best graphics.

Evolution of Nintendo Consoles

1889
Nintendo Founded

Fusajiro Yamauchi starts Nintendo in Kyoto, Japan — making handmade Hanafuda playing cards.

1977
Color TV-Game

Nintendo's first home gaming hardware. A set of dedicated consoles for simple TV games.

1983
NES

The Nintendo Entertainment System launches. Super Mario Bros. and Zelda help define gaming.

1989
Game Boy

Portable gaming goes mainstream. Tetris becomes one of the best-selling games of all time.

1990
SNES

16-bit graphics bring a huge upgrade. Super Mario World, Street Fighter II, and more.

1996
Nintendo 64

3D gaming arrives. Super Mario 64 and Ocarina of Time are still considered classics today.

2001
GameCube

Nintendo goes disc-based with a compact purple box. Beloved for its exclusive titles.

2006
Wii

Motion controls change everything. Wii Sports becomes one of the best-selling games ever.

2011
Nintendo 3DS

Glasses-free 3D gaming in your hands. A massive library of great handheld games.

2017
Nintendo Switch

The hybrid console that plays at home or on the go. Fastest-selling Nintendo console ever.

2019
Nintendo Switch Lite

A smaller, handheld-only version of the Switch at a lower price point.

2021
Nintendo Switch OLED

Upgraded with a larger OLED screen, better audio, and a wider adjustable stand.
